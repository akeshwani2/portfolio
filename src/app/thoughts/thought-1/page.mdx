>

growing up, i got really good at listening. like, really listening. i could reflect people back to themselves, say the right things, ask the right questions. people would leave feeling understood, and i’d leave feeling kind of… missing. like i was there, but not really.

at the time, it didn’t feel strange. it just felt normal. like that was my role.

when you grow up around parents who can’t handle their own emotions, love starts to feel like something you earn. you earn it by being useful, calm, steady. so you learn how to keep the peace. you read the room, you soften your reactions, you fix the vibe. and while you’re busy doing that, parts of you quietly start to disappear.

there’s a name for this: emotional parentification. it’s when the kid ends up managing the parent’s emotional world. role reversal basically. and it doesn’t just mess with your childhood, it changes how you operate as a person.

a lot of people who go through this end up with something called alexithymia, which is basically just struggling to know what you feel or how to say it. you’re so tuned into everyone else’s emotions that your own go blurry. you’re overloaded and numb at the same time. like feeling everything and nothing.

if you grow up in a place with lots of emotions but not much safety, your nervous system adapts. you develop high interpersonal sensitivity. you start watching people’s moods like they’re weather patterns. a slight change in tone, how someone sits down, how fast they reply, a weird pause — it all becomes data. your brain treats it like survival info.

this is tied to the “fawn” response. most people know fight, flight, and freeze. fawn is the fourth one. instead of attacking, running, or shutting down, you appease. you adjust. you make yourself as easy to handle as possible so you don’t get hurt or abandoned.

from the outside, it doesn’t look like trauma. it looks like empathy. it looks like kindness. people say you’re emotionally intelligent, mature, easy to be around. they love that you’re “chill,” that you understand them, that you don’t cause drama.

they don’t see that you’re editing yourself in real time so they stay comfortable. they don’t see what it costs you, because you don’t show them.

the pain of being “easy to love” doesn’t blow up all at once. it builds slowly. it shows up in the moments you stay quiet even though something bothered you. in the plans you say yes to when you actually want to say no. in how fast you say “whatever works for you,” even when it doesn’t.

the more you fawn, the easier it is to lose track of yourself. you’re so focused on scanning other people that you stop checking in with you. what do *you* actually want? what do *you* like? where do *you* end and they begin? over time, you start playing a role: the flexible one, the understanding one, the one who will adjust. that role keeps the peace, but it costs you honesty.

eventually, that role hardens into a mask. you forget how to take it off.

there’s also the brain part. social pain lights up similar regions as physical pain. the part of your brain that reacts to rejection can fire in a way that’s not that different from being physically hurt. so if you were taught that being “too much” risks love or connection, your brain steps in. it trims away the parts of you that might cause conflict. it teaches you to fit.

overtime you start to think that fitting in is the same thing as belonging... but is it?

after a while, it can start to feel like people love you *because* you don’t have needs. because you don’t ask for much. because you’re “low maintenance.” and under that is this quiet exhaustion. the automatic smile. the fast “it’s fine.” the stuff you never say out loud.

being easy to love starts to feel heavy. because each time you go along instead of telling the truth, you’re teaching yourself that your needs are extra. that your boundaries are flexible. that the full version of you is risky. and somewhere in the background, a question starts forming: if i stopped doing this… would they still want me?

people with anxious or disorganized attachment often end up hyper focused on other people’s emotions. they get really good at managing fears that aren’t theirs. over time, that can turn into constant fawning. you turn yourself into a service. you say sorry for taking up space. your body stays in survival mode, even on normal days. that makes it even harder to set a boundary without feeling guilty.

at some point, you learn: love means pleasing people. love means keeping everyone else okay, even when you’re not. and after years of that, you just get tired. you’ve been conditionally loved for so long that you kind of forget what it’s like to just exist without having to earn it.

it’s not that the love you get is fake. it’s just built on the parts of you that feel safe enough to show. your relationships start to reflect your performance, not your whole self. people care about you, but you don’t feel fully seen. you can be close to people and still feel weirdly far away. being easy to love can make you hard to actually know.

sometimes the reaction to all this is to swing to the opposite extreme. to become cold, distant, walled off. to act like you don’t need anyone, like nothing touches you. that can feel like armor for a while. like, if no one gets close, no one can ask you to perform.

but that’s still not freedom. it’s just a different costume.

being cold doesn’t fix the part of you that over accommodates. it just hides it. instead of disappearing into what other people want, you disappear behind your own walls. the softer parts of you — the messy, emotional, vulnerable ones — get pushed even deeper down. the ones that actually want to be seen.

the real work is quieter and harder: being honest.

not brutally honest with everyone, all the time. just honest enough that you stop abandoning yourself.

letting silence sit for a second instead of rushing to smooth it over. saying “i don’t know how i feel yet” instead of pretending you’re fine. admitting when something hurts. saying “that doesn’t really work for me” and not taking it back right away. letting yourself have needs, even small ones.

making space for a kind of love that doesn’t require you to disappear. love that doesn’t punish you for being complicated or emotional or unsure. love from people who want *you*, not just the calm, convenient version of you.

you’re not here to be someone’s emotional support system first and a person second. you’re a person. with your own fears, wants, confusion, feelings.

if you’re exhausted, it makes sense. masks are heavy. always being “easy” is heavy. you were never meant to live as just a reflection of what other people need.

love isn’t supposed to be constant shapeshifting.

and being easy to love should never be the price you pay to be loved at all.
